General Education course reflections
I’ve taken quite a few non-major-related courses at ISU. Three I’d like to highlight are HIST 390: world military history,
HDFS 239: Consumer issues, and LDST 322: Leadership in a diverse environment.
History 390 was a very interesting course. We covered most major wars in Europe and to a lesser extent elsewhere in the
world that occurred between the start of the Napoleonic era and the modern-day. I learned a lot about how militaries
evolve and adapt to different pressures. For example, after Napoleon conquered most of western Europe in the name of
France, he attempted to economically align himself with his neighbors. France at this time was spread thin and facing a lot
of rebellion but they had plans to siege the island of Britain by cutting off all trade with the mainland. Conquering every
meter of the coast was impossible so Napolean was forced to fight an economic blockade war. This was partially due to
the British specializing in naval warfare most of their history. Britain was always on the cutting edge of naval warfare
because if they weren’t they would be conquered swiftly. This logistical thread can be seen throughout history. World War
2 American tanks are widely agreed upon to not necessarily be the best tank fielded in the war but far and away the best
tank for the Americans. America needed a than that could be loaded on flatcars in the middle of the country, transported
by rail to the coast, loaded onto a ship, and sailed across the Atlantic before it saw any combat where European powers
like Germany simply needed to drive their tanks a few towns over before reaching the front lines. Well-planned operational
logistics always increase the chance a plan will succeed even if they end up being useless. Engineers need to keep in
mind that preparation and requirement identification is vital to any engineering job. A tank is no good if you can’t get it into
battle. This course also gave me a lot of good context surrounding major world powers, like France and Germany, and
how they fought to claim the borders they hold today. This context allows me to better understand foreign policy decisions
around the world.
HDFS 239 was an interesting class. It really drove home just how important budgeting is regardless of how much money.
We went in-depth with how living below your means is the easiest way to gain and maintain wealth, advice I intend to
follow after college. I also learned there are fiduciary advisors who are obligated to put your interests before all else and
are thus the best financial advisors out there. Budgeting is also necessary for engineering where you’ll be often asked
how much a project is going to cost and then held to that. I think consumer issues better apply to the general public,
though, where most people should learn to live more within their means so that the country can collectively worry less
about money.
LDST 322 has expanded my leadership capabilities. We’ve talked about how women are slightly statistically more likely to focus on
relationships during a project over goals and how either focus can be useful in certain situations. We’ve talked about how emotional
intelligence is key to good leadership in that it allows you to understand what your team needs and react to that. Finally, I had a gap
in my knowledge of African American history-filled. The lack of a unifying leader for the African American community after Dr. Martin
Luther King Jr. was always somewhat ignored in my teaching until this leadership class talked about multiple leaders that were
threatened and/or silenced before they could unify the African American community. This class will certainly help in engineering
reminding me to consider other viewpoints and how to approach possibly uncomfortable subjects. It’s also prepared me for the wider
world where there is always a desperate need for good leaders willing to look out for all their subordinates regardless of background.
