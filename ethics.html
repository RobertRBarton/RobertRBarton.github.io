Ethics
Ethics is an interesting topic. Every field has its own form of ethics, yet few fields focus more
than a few high-level committees on ethics, bar philosophy. I believe that humans generally understand
ethics for the most part. Nobody sets out to ruin lives and harm people, but sometimes we can be tempted
or distracted from the impacts of our actions.
Having a well-defined code of ethics can help someone understand what behavior they should
promote in themselves, but “a written code can only approximate what an unwritten code can do” (Brant,
37). The purpose of having and following a code of ethics is to be able to recognize unethical behavior in
yourself and others and understand that that behavior is unethical and should be avoided to maintain
professional credibility.
Personally, when I face any decision, I first consider the boy scout law. The law, a 12 pillar list of
ideals, which every boy scout quickly memorizes as it is recited at the beginning of every meeting. If I
determine a decision to be possibly unethical I determine who may be affected by this decision and focus
on limiting harm to unaware people. I would rather take the blame over my team taking it, I would rather
my team mess up than our company, and so on to the customer. I can understand making a mistake and
facing an ethical dilemma. I am frustrated by people with a responsibility that handoff that dilemma to
others.
In Cpr E 394, a computer engineering-focused ethics class at Iowa State University, we were
presented with four ethical issues faced in the modern world. We looked at the scandal of Volkswagen
cheating US emissions tests in 2009, the poorly designed Ford pinto that was cleared for production in
spite of glaring flaws, a murder case where police took an Amazon Echo as evidence possibly having
recorded a clue to the identity of the murderer, and a man constructing a database on anonymized data on
paramedic field injuries who was asked to include names and other identifying information. We discussed
these amongst our class online.
Our class didn’t come to a consensus per se, but I believe all of us agreed generally where the
ethical lines were drawn. However, if those lines were crossed the opinions on what the repercussions
should be varied. Some people jumped straight to a loss of job for all unethical behavior, others suggested
talking with a supervisor about the implications of the decision they were facing.
My whole class discussed these issues without really focusing on one main issue. We were also
presented with the “Virtue of Ethics” an article by William A. Brant about some supposedly overarching
common ethics, consisting of integrity, honesty, fidelity, charity, responsibility, and self-discipline. I
would argue that while honesty is important it can be detrimental to focus on. I suggest that honesty be
rephrased to trustworthiness, a small but important change. I believe it is important to come forward with
issues and not lie, but it is also important to be willing to not answer a question to maintain trust.
